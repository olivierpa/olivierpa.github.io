ILL NATION

It all started with a logo.
Meant to be the emblem for a streetwear brand, the symbol was designed to act as a reminiscence of the brand's name but also to play an important part in the uniqueness of the products.

In order to achieve these two main goals, I borrowed a very recognizable aspect common to all team sports: the jersey number.

As a result, the letters from "ill" were designed to form the letter "n" and the number "11".

the problem

Like all brands, Ill Nation needed a digital platform to sell its products.
The range of items being pretty narrow (different color variations of a t-shirt, a sweatshirt and a tank top), the website couldn't keep the same formula used by other retail competitors.

Ill Nation had to set itself apart by providing a different shopping experience and by finding a new way to engage its customers in the process.

the solution

The experience revolves around the idea of Ill Nation being a team. When retail websites will usually welcome you with a gender choice, often illustrated with professional models, this homepage will allow you to discover the items by browsing pictures from the "Ill community" (posted on social media).

The photos are linked to the item worn and each of them being available for both men and women, this choice can be made on the item description page thanks to a switch.

MÉTABOLISME

Have you ever wanted to know how the different stakeholders involved in a project felt about this very project? Métabolisme might be the right tool for you.

Based on the theories developed in the Semiotics Lab of Arts and Design part of the University Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne, this tool allows a project manager to visualize the agreements and disagreements concerning the current state of the project and to monitor the evolution of the thoughts of everyone involved.

the problem

We worked on a complete redesign of an existing tool.
Our tests of the previous version showed that the process was very unclear to users and that the whole experience felt unfinished and confusing.

We made the decision to create a completely new user flow that would contribute to a more streamlined experience. We focused on rethinking the way answers are collected and how the results are displayed.

the solution

We were able to simplify the whole process by implementing features that were more adapted to the situations the author of the inquiries usually faced when using the tool.

First, we allowed the user to send surveys by mail so the questions could be asked remotely at any time. We then made sure that the mails and surveys sent were clear enough to avoid confusion.

Last but not least, we redesigned the dashboard so the results could be evaluated on one page instead of four.

PARADES

Parades is a board game initially created to be part of an exhibit curated by a cancer survivor.

The goal was to provide an activity to visitors that would allow them to complete their experience and have a better grasp of a survivor's journey.

Intended for a public setting, it was necessary for the game to be simple and quick enough so the most people could get a chance to play.

the problem

Coming up with an original concept for this game was a task that provided more difficulties than expected.

Education and fun are often difficult to combine but adding this unsettling theme to the mix made things quite harder.

Nevertheless, we managed to find the right blend by relying on true stories (that ended well) and on a concept that encourage cooperation but also competition.

the solution

At each turn, the moderator shares with the players what happened in the patient’s life and how his condition has evolved. They can receive explanations about medical terms or get more details on the situation thanks to a card.

The goal is for them to put themselves in the medical team’s shoes by finding the right combination of cards (each representing a medical specialist) and to provide the right treatment as fast and accurately as possible until the patient is cured.

In order to spark more passionate and focused exchanges, we got rid of the traditional board and introduced a time limit.

The only board-like element is the life gauge of the patient that evolves depending on their answers and on events happening in the patients’ life.

Introducing a time limit could be seen as an hurdle for the communication, but as the players focused a lot more on the cards and on the information given to them, it only inspired them to talk, work together and pay more attention to the patient.

T.R.A.S.

Martinique (French West Indies) has been battling the invasion of a seaweed called sargassum for several years now.

To this day, the only tools used to get rid of this algae are excavators, which are damaging the beach and its fauna, or simple shovels and wheelbarrows, which are not nearly efficient enough.

As part of a design fiction course, I tried to come up with a way to tackle this issue and even make the best out of the situation.

the problem

As this project embodies my desire to see my island turn towards real innovation, instead of relying upon the same old temporary methods that only delay the problem, I decided to observe all the aspects of this invasion.

Sargassum had already had visible impacts on inhabitants and on the economy so the new tool that arises from this study should provide some answers to these subjects.

the solution

The new device is a fully automated robot capable of "eating" seaweed and storing it so it can be transformed later, for fuel or electricity.

Powered by solar energy, this robot also stands out thanks to its tortoise shape. Imitating nature to protect the environment, this friendly form can even attract tourists.
It also aims to be useful in the long-term by allowing people to interact with it through an app.

FUTURE ADS

There are some products we can hope will never come to exist... Here are ads for them!

More than ever, moving and still images seem to follow us wherever we go. Advertisements are a prime example of this phenomenon as it is clear how much influence they still have on some people's opinion and psyche.

My goal was to materialize what some would call nightmare scenarios to see if ads could really convince us to accept the worse.

the problem

In order to observe genuine reactions, both the ads and the products they displayed had to be plausible.
For each product, I combined technologies widely available to illustrate a future that could be closer than we think.

What would happen if the Amazon Dash Button meets smart connected clothes or when Tesla self-driving cars associate with VR headsets? What would your reaction be if you saw an ad promoting this?

the solution

The common theme with all those new products were the automation of everyday and personal actions. Here are some questions these ads ask:
- Would you wear Amazon clothes that launch a delivery to replace them once they get a little bit shabby?
- Can you entrust your Snapchat account to a drone following you and posting pictures depending on your facial expressions and your body temperature?
- How safe would you feel wearing a VR headset inside of a self-driving car?

MICRODOC

What would you do if you could see everything that's happening inside your body in real time? Microdoc is going to help you figure it out!

Taking place in a future where people get chips implanted so they can follow every internal changes at any moment (basically Black Mirror's Arkangel episode except we did this a year earlier), this project was created to see how far could a bad chatbot go when placed in role traditionally held by humans.

the problem

We created an interface that aimed to create an oppressing atmosphere for the user, thanks to a never ending flow of numbers and scientific terms going through the screen and frightening alerts.

Our research allowed us to create personas that embodied the principal types of people that we really needed to take into account to design a complete experience. These personas were essential for the construction of the user flow that helped us identify the moments our chatbot was going to interact with the users.

the solution

In this context, we wanted our chatbot to accentuate the anxious feeling users could feel after an alert by adding a different kind of layer to the experience.

What is a better to do that than by playing with emotions and retaining important information? That is the reason why we gave a sassy personality to our chatbot. It would ask questions to delay its answer, disrespect the way of life of the user and announce bad news by displaying riddles.
